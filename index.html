Goal 4

The workshop treats interpretability as an intervention tool. Understanding internal computations should support editing, debiasing, privacy-aware adaptation, uncertainty calibration, and inference-time monitoring for clinical safety.

Topic focus

Medical foundation-model architectures

The workshop covers the models that now matter most at MICCAI: medical vision transformers, diffusion models, and multimodal image-text or clinical foundation models.

Mechanistic question Where do clinically meaningful representations emerge across modalities and layers?
Clinical relevance These architectures increasingly power report generation, retrieval, triage, segmentation, and diagnosis support.
Typical methods Layer analysis, representation probes, activation studies, and cross-modal circuit tracing.
Topic focus

Sparse feature discovery and SAEs

One of the clearest routes to mechanistic understanding is to identify sparse, reusable internal features that correspond to anatomy, pathology, protocol artifacts, or shortcut cues.

Mechanistic question Can we discover interpretable features that correspond to clinically meaningful concepts?
Clinical relevance Feature-level understanding can reveal when a model uses pathology versus scanner or acquisition confounds.
Typical methods Dictionary learning, sparse autoencoders, feature visualization, ablation, and concept validation.
Topic focus

Circuits, shortcuts, and causal tracing

Rather than asking where attention was high, this topic asks which internal pathways causally produce correct predictions, harmful shortcuts, or sensitivity to domain shift.

Mechanistic question Which combinations of heads, layers, or features implement clinically relevant or spurious behavior?
Clinical relevance Circuit-level understanding can expose hidden reliance on site, protocol, text leakage, or artifact cues.
Typical methods Activation patching, causal mediation, intervention studies, and circuit discovery workflows.
Topic focus

Faithfulness and clinical meaning

Mechanistic results must be validated, not only visualized. This topic emphasizes how to benchmark explanations against trusted concepts, controlled tasks, and downstream clinical evidence.

Mechanistic question Do discovered features or circuits remain predictive and causal under controlled variation?
Clinical relevance Clinical adoption depends on evidence that interpretability findings are faithful and not storytelling.
Typical methods Concept alignment tests, synthetic tasks, robustness checks, reproducibility studies, and expert review.
Topic focus

Failure tracing and mechanistic debugging

Some of the most valuable work will explain why models fail: hallucinations in report generation, calibration mistakes, spurious correlations, and brittle multimodal reasoning.

Mechanistic question What internal states or pathways consistently precede known failure modes?
Clinical relevance Tracing failures internally can inform selective deployment, monitoring, and correction strategies.
Typical methods Error-conditioned activation analysis, counterfactual inputs, representation comparisons, and feature ablations.
Topic focus

Mechanistic model editing, steering, and debiasing

Once internal mechanisms are identified, the next step is intervention: change the behavior without retraining the entire model, or introduce targeted controls that improve robustness and fairness.

Mechanistic question Which internal features or weights can be modified to reduce errors or bias?
Clinical relevance Intervention-ready insight is essential when models operate in high-stakes, shifting environments.
Typical methods Feature steering, weight editing, targeted finetuning, debiasing interventions, and calibration-aware control.
Topic focus

Monitoring unsafe states and distribution shift

The deployment question is central: can internal signals warn us when a model is operating outside safe conditions, encountering novel shift, or about to produce low-trust outputs?

Mechanistic question Which internal signatures indicate unreliable predictions or OOD operating regimes?
Clinical relevance Monitoring is one of the most practical bridges from interpretability to deployment safety.
Typical methods Feature-level detectors, uncertainty signals, activation outlier analysis, and inference-time alarms.
Topic focus

Scalable open-source tooling for MedFM interpretability

Even strong methods will stall if they are too hard to reproduce. The workshop explicitly welcomes tooling that makes mechanistic analysis faster, cheaper, and more usable for the broader MICCAI community.

Mechanistic question How do we make workflows around features, circuits, and interventions practical at MedFM scale?
Clinical relevance Good tooling is what turns a niche technique into a community capability that can support translation.
Typical methods Open-source libraries, benchmark suites, visualization environments, and standardized evaluation pipelines.

Submission and review

Two-track submission with a clear, rigorous review path

The review plan is intentionally easy to understand at a glance: two submission tracks, double-blind OpenReview-based review, poster-first presentation, and oral selections for standout work.

Track 1

Proceedings track

Original work intended for the MICCAI Satellite Events joint Springer LNCS proceedings.

Track 2

Non-archival track

Extended abstracts, previously published work, or work under review elsewhere.

Review framework

How accepted work moves through the workshop

Double-blind review Each submission receives at least 2 reviews, and 3 when feasible.
Meta-review oversight Senior PC or area chairs synthesize feedback and support decisions.
Poster-first visibility All accepted work appears as a poster; a subset is selected for oral spotlight time.
COI safeguards Conflicted organizers do not handle submissions from the same institution or recent collaborators.

FAQ

Quick answers for readers, attendees, and prospective authors

Who should attend MI4MedFM?

Researchers in medical imaging and clinical AI, clinicians, interpretability and safety researchers, and industry practitioners working with medical foundation models.

What makes this workshop different from standard XAI or MedFM events?

Its emphasis is on internal mechanisms, causal analysis, clinically grounded validation, and intervention-ready insight rather than only post-hoc explanation or benchmark performance.

What kinds of submissions fit?

Original proceedings-track papers, as well as non-archival extended abstracts, previously published work, or work currently under review elsewhere.

How will accepted work be presented?

All accepted work will appear as posters, with a subset selected for oral presentation based on review quality and topic balance.
